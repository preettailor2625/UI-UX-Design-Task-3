Overview
For my third and final internship task assigned by CODTECH, I was required to choose a popular mobile application, identify a specific pain point in its existing user interface, and redesign it to provide a better, more intuitive user experience. For this task, I chose Spotify — one of the world's most widely used music streaming platforms with over 600 million active users globally. While Spotify is an excellent product in terms of its music library and recommendation engine, its mobile app has long suffered from a navigation experience that feels cluttered, inconsistent, and confusing — especially for new users. My redesign focused entirely on fixing this navigation problem while also giving the app a fresh, modern, and minimal visual identity.

Pain Point Identified
After carefully analysing the existing Spotify mobile app, the main pain point I identified was its poor and overcrowded navigation experience. The current app has five items crammed into the bottom navigation bar — Home, Search, Your Library, Premium, and Profile. Having Premium as a dedicated navigation tab feels forced and out of place, as it is essentially an advertisement embedded directly into the core navigation of the app. This makes the navigation bar feel cluttered and pushes more important features out of easy reach.
Beyond the navigation bar, the Your Library section presented another major problem. It mixes playlists, artists, albums, and podcasts all together in a single long list with no filtering or sorting options, making it extremely difficult for users to find what they are looking for quickly. The Search screen also had issues — it jumped directly into a grid of genre categories without showing the user their recent searches, which is one of the most commonly expected features in any search experience.
The Now Playing screen further added to the confusion by placing all controls — including shuffle, repeat, heart, lyrics, share, queue, and device — all on the same visual level with no grouping or hierarchy, making it hard to quickly find the primary playback controls.

Design Decisions and Changes Made
1. Simplified Bottom Navigation
In my redesign, I reduced the bottom navigation bar from five tabs to four — Home, Search, Library, and Profile. This immediately removes the clutter and eliminates the awkward Premium tab from the navigation. The Premium upgrade path is better suited to a settings or profile screen, not the main navigation. Each icon in the redesigned nav bar is cleaner, better spaced, and highlighted in Spotify's signature green when active, making the current section immediately obvious to the user.
2. Personalised Home Screen
The redesigned Home screen opens with a warm personalised greeting — "Good Morning" — along with horizontal filter pills that let users switch between For You, Recently Played, and New Releases content. This gives users immediate control over what type of content they see without having to scroll endlessly. The quick-access playlist cards are redesigned as rounded, card-style components with clean typography that is far easier to read than the original dark flat tiles.
3. Improved Search Screen
The redesigned Search screen now shows the user's recent searches as interactive chip tags at the very top, right below the search bar. This is a simple but powerful improvement because returning users almost always want to search for something they looked up recently. Below that, the browse categories are reorganised into soft pastel-coloured cards with matching icons for each category — Music, Podcasts, Live Events, Charts, Mood, and Trending — making them easier to scan and visually distinguish from each other.
4. Filtered Library Screen
The redesigned Library screen introduces a filter pill bar at the top with options — All, Playlists, Artists, Albums — allowing users to instantly narrow down their saved content by type. A search bar is also added inside the Library so users can find a specific saved playlist or artist without having to scroll through everything. Each library item is displayed as a clean white card with a rounded album art thumbnail, title, and subtitle, giving the entire screen a much more organised and breathable layout.
5. Redesigned Now Playing Screen
The Now Playing screen received the most visual transformation. The background shifts from pitch black to a light, airy tone that reduces eye strain during daytime use. The album artwork is displayed larger with rounded corners and a soft border. The heart button is moved right next to the song title and artist name, making it far more accessible. The secondary controls — lyrics, share, queue, device — are grouped together in their own row above the main playback controls, creating a clear visual hierarchy. The play button is now enclosed in a bold dark circle, making it the unmistakable focal point of the entire screen.

What I Learned
This project helped me develop a deeper understanding of how UI design decisions directly impact the user experience. I learned how to think critically about navigation architecture, information hierarchy, and visual grouping. Working in Figma, I practised creating reusable components, applying consistent color styles and text styles, and building a clickable prototype that simulates real app interactions with screen-to-screen transitions. The before and after comparison clearly demonstrates the improvements made and shows how thoughtful redesign can solve real usability problems without changing the core functionality of the product.

Conclusion
The Spotify redesign was a challenging yet deeply rewarding task. By focusing on one specific pain point — poor navigation — and addressing it systematically across all four key screens of the app, I was able to produce a prototype that feels noticeably cleaner, faster to navigate, and more enjoyable to use. The light and minimal theme gives the redesign a modern identity while keeping Spotify's brand colour, the iconic green, as the primary accent throughout. This project has significantly strengthened my Figma skills and my overall approach to mobile UI/UX design thinking.
